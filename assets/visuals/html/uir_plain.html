UIR in Plain English

UIR is robot planning in the physical world. The key difference from abstract planning is geometry, sensing, uncertainty, motion constraints, and coordination between robots.

1. Name the robot problem.Control, path, exploration, tasks, or sampling.
2. Name the space.World, grid, graph, or configuration space.
3. Give the method.A*, D*, frontier, allocation, PRM/RRT.

Robot architectures

How sensing, planning, and acting are organized.

  • Hierarchical: sense-plan-act.
  • Reactive: fast behavior from perception.
  • Hybrid: deliberative planner plus reactive layer.

Path and motion planning

Move from start to goal without collision.

  • Configuration space represents robot poses.
  • Grid/graph planning uses A* and variants.
  • D*/D* Lite replan when the map changes.

Exploration

Plan to gain information, not just reach a known goal.

  • Frontier = boundary between known free and unknown space.
  • Information gain and entropy score useful observations.
  • Exploration balances travel cost and new knowledge.

Multi-robot and multi-goal

Assign tasks and order visits.

  • Centralized methods optimize globally but scale poorly.
  • Decentralized methods scale but may be less optimal.
  • Robotic TSP adds geometry and motion constraints.

Sampling planners

Avoid explicit full-space search in continuous spaces.

  • PRM builds a roadmap.
  • RRT grows a tree toward random samples.
  • RRT*/PRM* add asymptotic optimality.

How to answer

Always mention physical constraints.

  • What is the state/configuration?
  • What is known vs. unknown?
  • What guarantee does the planner have?
Exam sentence: Motion planning usually works in configuration space, where each point represents a full robot pose and obstacles become forbidden regions.

Three traps

  • Path planning ignores some motion constraints; motion planning includes geometry/kinematics.
  • Probabilistic completeness does not mean guaranteed success in finite time.
  • RRT is not asymptotically optimal; RRT* is.
