TAL in Plain English

TAL is about sorting problems by what machines can solve, how fast they can solve them, how much memory they need, and which problems cannot be solved at all.

1. Identify the world.Time, space, randomness, or decidability?
2. Name the class.P, NP, PSPACE, RP, RE, decidable...
3. Give the reason.Algorithm, certificate, reduction, or contradiction.

Complexity basics

What resources does an algorithm use?

  • Time = number of steps.
  • Space = working memory, usually not counting read-only input.
  • Correctness = invariant for truth, variant for termination.

Turing machines

The formal model behind algorithms.

  • Deterministic: one next move.
  • Nondeterministic: many branches; accept if one branch accepts.
  • Multitape changes speed polynomially, not the big classes.

P, NP, reductions

Can we solve it fast, or verify a solution fast?

  • P: polynomial-time solving.
  • NP: polynomial-time verification/certificate.
  • NP-hard: everything in NP reduces to it.

Space and randomness

Memory-bounded and probability-bounded computation.

  • PSPACE: polynomial memory.
  • Savitch: NPSPACE = PSPACE.
  • RP/co-RP/ZPP differ by one-sided or zero error.

Undecidability

Some yes/no questions have no always-correct terminating algorithm.

  • Decidable: halts with yes/no on every input.
  • RE: halts on yes instances, may loop on no.
  • Halting problem: classic contradiction by diagonalization.

How to answer

Most TAL answers are classification plus proof idea.

  • Define the class/problem.
  • State the inclusion or theorem.
  • Use reduction, certificate, simulation, or diagonalization.
Exam sentence: NP means solutions can be verified in polynomial time; NP-complete problems are in NP and at least as hard as every NP problem by polynomial reduction.

Three traps

  • NP does not mean "not polynomial"; P may still equal NP.
  • Reduce from a known hard problem to prove your target hard.
  • Semi-decidable is not the same as decidable.
