Module 1

Empirical risk, generalization, consistency, and VC dimension

ERM chooses the hypothesis with the lowest training loss. The exam question is really about when this is trustworthy: Hoeffding for fixed hypotheses, a union bound for finite classes, and VC dimension for infinite classes.

Committee prioritySolid focus

ERM and VC theory support the heavier ML questions signaled by Gustav Šir, especially when an answer must justify why a learned classifier generalizes.

  • risk versus empirical risk
  • Hoeffding and finite-class union bounds
  • VC dimension and consistency caveats

Use this when you want the finite-class union-bound story in a course-note form: learnability, finite classes, PAC learning, uniform convergence, and VC dimension.

descriptionOpen MIT notes

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • Prediction examples are drawn from an unknown distribution; a hypothesis h is judged by a loss such as 0/1, squared, hinge, or logistic loss.
  • True risk is expected loss on the data distribution; empirical risk is average loss on the sample; ERM minimizes empirical risk over H.
  • Hoeffding bounds the train-true deviation for a fixed bounded-loss hypothesis.
  • For finite H, union bound gives R(h) <= Rhat(h) + sqrt(log(2|H|/delta)/(2m)) with high probability.
  • For infinite classes, VC dimension replaces log |H|; typical rates look like sqrt((d + log(1/delta))/m).
  • Statistical consistency means risk converges to the best achievable risk as sample size grows, assuming uniform convergence, optimization, and a suitable model class.

Remember: training loss is what you can measure; true risk is what the examiner cares about.

Module 2

Support vector machines, hinge loss, and kernels

SVMs are regularized ERM with a geometric memory hook: maximize the margin. The soft-margin version allows violations, and the kernel trick swaps dot products for feature-space inner products.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Šir's machine-learning signal and student-report overlap make SVMs one of the highest-yield SSU answers.

  • hard and soft margin formulations
  • hinge loss as regularized ERM
  • kernel trick and support-vector prediction

Fallback link

SVM polynomial kernel - StatQuest (7:15)

Fallback link

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A linear SVM uses f(x) = w^T x + b and constraints y_i(w^T x_i + b) >= 1.
  • The geometric margin is 2 / ||w||, so the hard-margin primal minimizes 1/2 ||w||^2 subject to correct margin constraints.
  • Soft margin introduces slack xi_i >= 0 and minimizes 1/2 ||w||^2 + C sum xi_i.
  • The soft-margin objective is equivalent to regularized ERM with hinge loss max(0, 1 - y f(x)).
  • C trades larger margin against training violations.
  • The kernel trick replaces x_i^T x_j by K(x_i,x_j) = phi(x_i)^T phi(x_j); prediction depends only on support vectors.

Remember: margin controls geometry, C controls violations, kernels control the feature space.

Module 3

Maximum likelihood estimation and EM

MLE chooses parameters that make the observed data most likely. EM is the rescue plan when hidden variables put a log around a sum: alternate posterior responsibilities and parameter refitting.

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Likelihood and latent-variable fitting are core statistical-learning foundations behind the ML-heavy committee signal.

  • likelihood and log-likelihood maximization
  • MLE examples and consistency assumptions
  • EM lower bound, E-step, M-step, and local optima
70-90 min

Watch first

Maximum likelihood, clearly explained - StatQuest (6:12)

Fallback link

EM algorithm: how it works - Victor Lavrenko (7:53)

Fallback link

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • Likelihood is L(theta) = product_i p_theta(x_i); log-likelihood is sum_i log p_theta(x_i); MLE maximizes it.
  • Bernoulli MLE is the sample mean; Gaussian mean MLE is the sample mean.
  • An estimator is consistent if it converges in probability to the true parameter as sample size grows.
  • With latent z, likelihood has log sum_z p_theta(x,z), which is hard to optimize directly.
  • EM uses Jensen's inequality to build a lower bound; the gap is a KL divergence to the true posterior.
  • E-step sets q_i(z) = p_theta_old(z | x_i); M-step maximizes expected complete-data log-likelihood; likelihood increases monotonically but can stop at a local optimum.

Remember: MLE fits observed data; EM adds soft guesses for hidden data before fitting again.

Backpropagation, intuitively - 3Blue1Brown (12:47)

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A neuron computes z = w^T x + b, then activation a = sigma(z).
  • Common activations include sigmoid, tanh, ReLU, leaky ReLU, and softmax at multiclass output.
  • A feedforward layer is h^(l) = sigma(W^(l) h^(l-1) + b^(l)).
  • Architectures include MLPs, CNNs, RNN/LSTM/GRU, residual networks, and autoencoders.
  • Backprop computes delta^(l) = dL/dz^(l), gradients dL/dW^(l) = delta^(l)(a^(l-1))^T, and dL/db^(l) = delta^(l).
  • The backward recursion multiplies by the next layer's weights and the local derivative: delta^(l) = (W^(l+1)^T delta^(l+1)) * sigma'(z^(l)).

Remember: a delta is the blame arriving at a layer before its activation.

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • Full gradient descent updates theta <- theta - eta grad L(theta); SGD estimates the gradient from one example or a minibatch.
  • Minibatch SGD trades noisy but cheap updates against exact but expensive full gradients.
  • Practical improvements include momentum, Adam/RMSProp, learning-rate schedules, weight decay, early stopping, data augmentation, and normalization.
  • Bad initialization causes vanishing or exploding activations/gradients; Xavier is common for tanh/sigmoid-like activations and He for ReLU.
  • CNNs use convolution filters with local connectivity, weight sharing, translation equivariance, channels, and pooling/striding for downsampling.
  • Deep nets can overfit; exam answers should mention train/validation split, regularization, augmentation, early stopping, dropout, weight decay, and architecture constraints.

Remember: optimization makes training loss go down; regularization is what keeps validation loss honest.

Exam-risk ML fundamentals

Decision trees, ensembles, metrics, and bias/variance

  • Bias is systematic underfitting error; variance is sensitivity to the training sample. Regularization, simpler models, and more data reduce variance; richer models reduce bias.
  • A decision tree recursively splits features to reduce impurity such as entropy/information gain or Gini impurity. It overfits easily unless depth, leaf size, pruning, or validation control it.
  • Bagging trains models on bootstrap samples and averages/votes to reduce variance; random forests add feature subsampling to decorrelate trees.
  • Boosting trains weak learners sequentially with more focus on previous mistakes; it can reduce bias but is sensitive to noise and needs regularization.
  • Classification metrics come from the confusion matrix: accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, F1, ROC-AUC, and PR-AUC. Use precision/recall/F1 when classes are imbalanced.

Lower confidence

SVM margin classifier

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Direct reported-drill overlap with a high-signal ML topic.

  • separable versus nonseparable SVM
  • margin drawing, slack, and hinge loss
  • kernel explanation and QP distinction

Context: Bachelor AI/RPZ-style reports and one OI AI report asked for SVMs, separable and nonseparable cases, kernels, margin drawings, and the optimization type.

Show model answer

A linear SVM classifies by sign(w^T x+b). In the separable hard-margin case it solves min 1/2||w||^2 subject to y_i(w^T x_i+b) >= 1, maximizing geometric margin 2/||w||.

  • For nonseparable data, add slack xi_i >= 0 and minimize 1/2||w||^2 + C sum xi_i with y_i(w^T x_i+b) >= 1-xi_i.
  • This is regularized hinge-loss ERM; C trades margin size against violations.
  • The optimization is quadratic programming, not linear programming, because of ||w||^2.
  • Kernels replace dot products by K(x,z)=phi(x)^T phi(z), giving nonlinear boundaries while prediction depends on support vectors.
  • In the drawing, support vectors lie on or inside the margin; maximizing margin improves robustness but kernels and large C can overfit.

Neural network design and backprop

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Reported questions match the committee-heavy neural-network mechanics almost one-to-one.

  • one-hidden-layer binary classifier design
  • backpropagation and SGD training story
  • overfitting, universal approximation, and output extensions

Context: Bachelor AI/RPZ-style reports asked how neural networks work, how backprop trains them, how to extend output layers, and how to design a binary classifier from three features.

Show model answer

Use inputs x in R^3, a hidden layer h=sigma(W_1x+b_1), and an output such as p=sigmoid(w_2^T h+b_2) for binary classification. Train with binary cross-entropy or another suitable classification loss.

  • Backprop computes forward activations, loss, output error, hidden-layer errors by the chain rule, then updates weights with SGD/minibatches or Adam.
  • For multiple outputs, replace the scalar output by several neurons, typically softmax plus cross-entropy for single-label multiclass or independent sigmoid outputs for multilabel tasks.
  • Perfect training or test classification is not guaranteed: data may be noisy, nonseparable, insufficiently modeled, or optimization may find only a local/minibatch-dependent solution.
  • Universal approximation says sufficiently wide networks can approximate broad classes of continuous functions, but it does not guarantee good generalization or easy training.
  • Prevent overfitting with validation, early stopping, weight decay, dropout, data augmentation, simpler architecture, and enough data.

Oral skeleton

One clean answer path

Use this as the shape of a spoken exam answer. It is deliberately compact: definitions first, formulas second, caveats third.

1. ERM: "We choose a hypothesis from H by minimizing empirical loss. The target is true risk, an expectation over the unknown distribution. Hoeffding controls the gap for one hypothesis; a union bound controls all hypotheses in finite H; VC dimension controls infinite binary classes. Consistency means the learned risk converges to the best achievable risk under suitable assumptions."

2. SVM: "A linear SVM maximizes margin, equivalently minimizes 1/2 ||w||^2 under margin constraints. Soft margin adds slack and becomes regularized hinge-loss ERM. In the dual, examples appear through dot products, so kernels allow nonlinear decision boundaries. Only support vectors affect prediction."

3. MLE and EM: "MLE maximizes log-likelihood. With latent variables, likelihood contains a log of a sum, so EM optimizes a Jensen lower bound. The E-step sets responsibilities to the posterior under old parameters; the M-step maximizes expected complete-data log-likelihood. It increases likelihood monotonically but can converge locally."

4. Neural networks: "Layers compose affine maps and nonlinear activations. Backprop is the chain rule: compute output error, propagate deltas backward, and form gradients for weights and biases. Training uses SGD or minibatches with learning-rate choices and optimizers."

5. Deep-training details: "CNNs exploit local connectivity and weight sharing; initialization such as Xavier or He keeps signals stable; regularization such as dropout, weight decay, augmentation, validation, and early stopping fights overfitting."

6. Nearby ML drills: "If the examiner pivots to classical classifiers, I can define decision trees by impurity-reducing splits, explain pruning against overfitting, compare bagging/random forests with boosting, and choose metrics from the confusion matrix instead of relying on accuracy alone."
