Domain independent planning

PUI is the art of turning a domain description into search: represent states and actions, estimate distance-to-goal with relaxations or abstractions, then switch from sequences to policies when actions become nondeterministic or probabilistic. Keep the oral answer anchored on one question: what problem did we relax, abstract, or sample?

Module 1

Planning tasks, STRIPS/SAS, and heuristic search

Classical planning is deterministic, fully observable, and static. Once the representation is fixed, the planner searches a huge implicit graph whose nodes are states and whose edges are applicable actions.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Komenda is directly aligned with automated planning, and reports emphasize that formalizing the task before algorithms is a major examiner expectation.

  • formal planning/search tuple, classical assumptions, and implicit state graph
  • STRIPS applicability/successor formula and SAS finite-domain representation
  • A*, admissibility, consistency, open/closed handling, and duplicate states
70-90 min

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A general planning/search problem can be stated as (S,A,gamma,c,s0,G): states, actions, transition/successor function, action costs, initial state, and goal test.
  • A classical planning task has finite states, deterministic actions, full observability, known initial state, a goal condition, and a static domain.
  • In STRIPS, Pi = (F,A,I,G); actions have preconditions, add effects, and delete effects.
  • PDDL is the usual syntax for writing STRIPS-like domains and problems: predicates/types/actions in the domain file, objects/initial state/goal in the problem file.
  • An action is applicable when pre(a) subset s; the successor is (s \ del(a)) union add(a).
  • SAS replaces many mutually exclusive propositional facts by one finite-domain variable, often making the representation smaller.
  • Satisficing planning wants any valid plan; optimal planning wants minimum cost or length and must prove no cheaper plan exists.
  • BFS handles unit costs, uniform-cost handles positive costs, greedy best-first uses only h, and A* uses g + h.
  • A* implementation answer: open priority queue by f=g+h, best known g per state, parent pointers for reconstruction, and closed/reopen policy depending on heuristic consistency.
  • Admissible means never overestimate; consistent means each estimate respects one-step triangle inequality.

Remember: a planner searches states; STRIPS only tells it which action edges exist.

Module 2

Delete relaxation and abstraction heuristics

Planning heuristics usually solve an easier problem. Delete relaxation removes negative interactions; abstraction projects the task into a smaller exact problem.

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Heuristics are a high-potential planning follow-up because they test whether you know exactly which easier problem each estimate solves.

  • delete relaxation and facts-only-accumulate intuition
  • hmax, hadd, hff, strength, and admissibility
  • projection abstractions, pattern databases, and merge-and-shrink

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • Delete relaxation sets every delete list to empty, so facts only accumulate and the relaxed task may allow impossible plans.
  • hmax propagates fact costs with max over preconditions and max over goals; it is admissible but often weak.
  • hadd replaces max by sum, so it is more informative but can double-count shared actions and is not admissible.
  • hff builds a relaxed planning graph and extracts one relaxed plan greedily; it is useful for satisficing search but not admissible.
  • An abstraction maps concrete states into abstract states and uses exact abstract distance as a heuristic.
  • Pattern databases keep only selected SAS variables; larger patterns are stronger but exponentially larger.
  • Merge-and-shrink merges atomic abstractions and shrinks them to keep memory manageable.

Remember: heuristics are useful because they solve an easier planning problem and reuse its distance.

Module 3

Landmarks, LM-Cut, linear programming heuristics, and portfolios

Landmarks are necessary achievements. LM-Cut turns necessary relaxed action cuts into an admissible cost estimate; LP heuristics express necessary plan conditions as linear constraints.

Committee priorityHeavy focus

These are exam-rich planning heuristics because they require precise definitions, a repeatable algorithm story, and a lower-bound/admissibility argument.

  • fact/action/formula landmarks and ordering relations
  • LM-Cut loop, relaxed action cuts, and cost subtraction
  • operator-counting/state-equation LP and potential heuristics

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A landmark is a fact, action, or formula that every valid plan must achieve or use.
  • Natural, greedy necessary, and reasonable orders describe how landmarks should appear relative to each other.
  • Landmarks can be found by relaxed planning graph analysis, backchaining from goals, necessary predecessors, and disjunctive choices.
  • LM-Cut repeatedly computes hmax, finds a relaxed action cut every relaxed plan must cross, adds the minimum cut cost, subtracts it from cut actions, and repeats until relaxed goal cost is zero.
  • State-equation heuristics use nonnegative action-count variables and necessary net-change constraints, then minimize action cost.
  • Potential heuristics assign weights to facts or SAS values and enforce Bellman-like action inequalities, giving admissible and consistent heuristics.
  • Planning portfolios combine heuristics and planners because performance is strongly domain-dependent.

Remember: landmarks are not advice; they are achievements every real plan is forced to pass through.

Lab

LM-Cut cost intuition

Each row is a cut: every relaxed plan must use at least one action in that row. LM-Cut may safely add the cheapest action cost in the cut, then reduce all actions in that cut by that amount.

Why: adding the minimum cut cost counts unavoidable progress without charging too much.

Module 4

Nondeterministic and probabilistic planning

When actions have several possible outcomes, a single sequence is fragile. The answer becomes a policy, and the strength of the policy depends on whether outcomes are adversarial/nondeterministic or probabilistic.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Planning under uncertainty is a direct high-potential extension of classical planning: the examiner can ask exactly when a sequence becomes a reactive policy.

  • weak, strong, and strong cyclic policies for nondeterministic planning
  • MDP tuple, Bellman backup, value iteration, and policy extraction
  • POMDP belief states and partial observability
75-95 min

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • In nondeterministic planning, an action maps a state to a set of possible successor states; a solution is usually a policy pi:S -> A.
  • A weak plan has at least one successful execution; a strong plan guarantees all executions reach the goal in finite time.
  • A strong cyclic plan allows loops but requires all fair executions to eventually reach the goal.
  • AND/OR search separates planner choices at OR nodes from environment outcomes at AND nodes.
  • Probabilistic planning attaches probabilities P(s'|s,a) and becomes an MDP (S,A,P,R,gamma).
  • Value iteration repeatedly applies the Bellman optimality backup; stochastic shortest path planning often minimizes expected cost to terminal goals.
  • POMDPs maintain belief states, update beliefs after observations, and are much harder than fully observable MDPs.

Remember: uncertainty changes the answer from one sequence to a policy that can react.

Module 5

Monte-Carlo Tree Search and UCT

MCTS is online, sample-based planning. It does not solve the whole MDP up front; it spends simulations around the current decision and balances promising actions against uncertain actions.

Committee prioritySolid focus

MCTS is useful when connected to planning under uncertainty, but it should not crowd out the formal planning, heuristic, MDP, and POMDP spine.

  • selection, expansion, rollout, and backpropagation
  • UCT exploitation/exploration rule
  • online sample-based planning with a generative model
55-75 min

Watch first

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • MCTS builds an asymmetric tree by repeated simulations from the current state.
  • One iteration has selection, expansion, simulation/rollout, and backpropagation.
  • UCT chooses the action with average value plus an exploration bonus C sqrt(ln N(s) / N(s,a)).
  • The average-value term exploits actions that look good; the bonus explores actions with few visits.
  • MCTS is useful when a generative model can sample next states and rewards but exhaustive dynamic programming is too expensive.
  • It is anytime: more rollouts generally improve the decision, but the planner can act after a fixed computation budget.

Remember: UCT chooses between what currently looks good and what has not been tried enough.

Show model answer

Start with the model: (S,A,gamma,c,s0,G) for states, applicable actions, successor/transition function, action cost, initial state, and goal test or goal set. A deterministic plan is an action sequence from s0 to a goal state.

Lower confidence

A* implementation details

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Multiple reported search prompts focus on implementation details, so this drill protects against stopping at the A* slogan.

  • open priority queue by f=g+h and parent reconstruction
  • best known g, duplicate detection, closed set, and reopening
  • admissible versus consistent heuristic guarantees

Context: Bachelor AI/ZUI reports on road networks, 15-puzzle, and search pushed for priority queues, generated states, already-seen states, admissibility, and consistency.

Show model answer

A* stores generated frontier states in an open priority queue ordered by f(s)=g(s)+h(s), where g is cost so far and h estimates remaining cost. It also stores best known g values and parent/action pointers.

  • When a successor is generated, ignore it if an equal or better g is already known; otherwise update it in open or reopen it.
  • A closed set records expanded states; with a consistent heuristic, closed states never need reopening.
  • Admissible means h never overestimates optimal remaining cost, giving optimality for tree search and graph search with proper reopening.
  • Consistent means h(s) <= c(s,a,s') + h(s'), which makes f nondecreasing along paths.
  • If h=0, A* is uniform-cost search/Dijkstra on nonnegative-cost graphs.
Lower confidence

Uninformed vs informed search

Context: Bachelor AI/ZUI reports asked for DFS, IDDFS, BFS, A*, formal state spaces, finite-solution conditions, vacuum world, road networks, and 15-puzzle examples.

Show model answer

First formalize states, actions, successor function, costs, initial state, and goal. Uninformed search uses only this model; informed search also uses a heuristic estimate of distance or cost to goal.

  • BFS is complete for finite branching and finite shallowest solution depth, and optimal for unit costs.
  • DFS is memory-light but not complete in infinite-depth spaces and not optimal; with finite spaces and cycle checking it can find a solution if one exists.
  • IDDFS combines DFS memory with BFS depth-order completeness for unit-cost depth.
  • UCS is complete and optimal for positive costs; A* adds h and is optimal with admissible/consistent handling.
  • For 15-puzzle, use compact board representation and admissible heuristics such as misplaced tiles or Manhattan distance; for vacuum world, define dirt/location states and action costs explicitly.
Lower confidence

Planning under uncertainty as MDP

Committee priorityHeavy focus

The reported MDP prompt is a direct planning-under-uncertainty bridge: replace a fixed plan with a policy and state the Bellman machinery cleanly.

  • MDP components S,A,P,R/cost and discount or terminal goals
  • policy and value function instead of one action sequence
  • Bellman backup, value iteration, and policy iteration

Context: An OI AI report asked about MDPs with a worked example; this overlaps PUI when deterministic plans become policies under uncertainty.

Show model answer

An MDP is a planning model with probabilistic outcomes: (S,A,P,R,gamma) or costs, where P(s'|s,a) gives transition probabilities and rewards/costs evaluate outcomes. The solution is a policy pi(s), not a fixed action sequence.

  • The value of a policy is expected discounted or total return from a state.
  • Bellman optimality: V*(s)=max_a [R(s,a)+gamma sum_s' P(s'|s,a)V*(s')].
  • Value iteration repeatedly applies Bellman backups; policy iteration alternates evaluation and improvement.
  • This is PUI-relevant because uncertainty turns search over paths into computation of contingent actions for each reachable state.

Oral answer skeleton

Use this as the final answer spine. The examiner can interrupt anywhere, and you still have a clean next sentence.

Say it

  1. Start with classical planning assumptions and say a plan is a sequence of deterministic actions from initial state to goal.
  2. If the prompt is informal, define (S,A,gamma,c,s0,G) before mentioning any algorithm.
  3. Define STRIPS formally, give the applicability and successor formula, then contrast SAS finite-domain variables.
  4. Mention PDDL as the domain/problem syntax for STRIPS-like models, then explain satisficing vs optimal planning.
  5. Give the search menu: BFS, uniform-cost, greedy best-first, A*. For A*, include open priority queue, closed/best-g bookkeeping, and reopen conditions.
  6. State admissible and consistent heuristics, then explain delete relaxation.
  7. Compare hmax, hadd, and hff by formula idea and admissibility.
  8. Explain abstraction heuristics, projection/pattern databases, and merge-and-shrink.
  9. Define landmarks, landmark orders, and LM-Cut's repeated relaxed-action cuts.
  10. Explain state-equation LP heuristics, potential heuristics, and why portfolios are practical.
  11. Switch to uncertainty: weak, strong, strong cyclic plans and AND/OR search.
  12. Finish with MDP value iteration, POMDP belief states, and MCTS/UCT as sample-based online planning.
