Module 1

Normal-form games, dominance, mixed strategies, and Nash equilibrium

A normal-form game is a compact snapshot of simultaneous strategic choice: players, actions, and utilities. Nash equilibrium says every chosen strategy is a best response to the others.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Kroupa and Komenda align strongly with game-theoretic MAS foundations, so the examiner can easily start from normal form and drill equilibrium vocabulary.

  • normal-form game tuple, pure and mixed strategies
  • dominance and iterated deletion caveats
  • Nash equilibrium, best responses, and mixed-strategy indifference
65-85 min

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A normal-form game is (N, (A_i), (u_i)): players, action sets, and utility functions over action profiles.
  • A pure strategy is a single action; a mixed strategy is a probability distribution over actions.
  • Expected utility sums payoff times the product of independent mixed-strategy probabilities.
  • Strict dominance means another strategy is better against every opponent action; weak dominance allows ties and at least one strict improvement.
  • Iterated removal of strictly dominated strategies is order-independent; weak-dominance removal can depend on the order.
  • A Nash equilibrium is stable against unilateral deviations, but it may be inefficient, non-unique, or only mixed.

Remember: Nash only checks one player switching alone; it does not check group moves or total welfare.

Module 2

Zero-sum LP, minimax, correlated equilibrium, and Stackelberg commitment

Zero-sum games have clean optimization structure. Correlated equilibrium and Stackelberg equilibrium are useful alternatives to Nash when recommendations or commitment change what agents can coordinate on.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

This is a compact way for the committee to connect MAS with optimization: zero-sum LP/minimax, then alternative equilibrium concepts when timing or coordination changes.

  • two-player zero-sum minimax theorem and primal/dual LP
  • correlated equilibrium obedience inequalities
  • Stackelberg commitment and security-game interpretation
55-75 min

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • In a two-player zero-sum matrix game, player 1 has payoff matrix A and player 2 has payoff -A.
  • The minimax theorem says max_x min_y x^T A y = min_y max_x x^T A y.
  • Player 1's LP maximizes value v subject to each column giving expected payoff at least v, probabilities summing to one, and nonnegativity.
  • General finite-game Nash computation includes support enumeration, Lemke-Howson, complementarity methods, and PPAD-completeness in general.
  • Correlated equilibrium uses a mediator recommending actions; no player wants to deviate after seeing the recommendation. It is LP-computable.
  • Stackelberg equilibrium is leader commitment followed by follower best response; security games are the standard example.

Remember: zero-sum has one shared game value; correlated and Stackelberg change coordination or timing.

Module 3

Extensive-form games, information sets, strategies, sequence form, and regret

Extensive-form games add time and information to strategic choice. The exam wants both the representation vocabulary and the solution algorithms: backward induction, sequence-form LP, and regret minimization/CFR.

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Extensive form is slightly less central than normal-form and coalitional material, but it is a natural follow-up when the examiner asks how sequential decisions change equilibrium reasoning.

  • game trees, information sets, strategies, and perfect recall
  • subgame-perfect equilibrium and backward induction
  • sequence form and regret/CFR for large zero-sum games
70-95 min

Yale information sets segment - Ben Polak (18:56)

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • An EFG has decision nodes, player labels, actions on edges, chance nodes, terminal utilities, and information sets.
  • Perfect information means singleton information sets; imperfect information means a player cannot distinguish some nodes.
  • Perfect recall means players do not forget their own previous actions or information.
  • A pure strategy specifies an action at every information set; a behavioral strategy randomizes locally at each information set.
  • With perfect recall, Kuhn's theorem gives realization equivalence between mixed and behavioral strategies.
  • Subgame-perfect equilibrium requires Nash behavior in every subgame and removes noncredible threats; backward induction solves finite perfect-information games.
  • Sequence form represents realization probabilities and gives compact LPs for two-player zero-sum perfect-recall EFGs.
  • Regret minimization converges to coarse correlated equilibrium generally and to Nash average strategies in two-player zero-sum games; CFR decomposes regret by information sets.

Remember: an extensive-form strategy must specify actions even at information sets that are never reached.

Module 4

Coalitional games, core stability, Shapley value, and voting power

Coalitional games switch from strategic actions to group value. The two exam lenses are stability, via the core, and fair allocation, via the Shapley value and voting-power indices.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Coalitions are a high-yield MAS committee topic because they test whether you can switch from unilateral equilibrium to group stability and fair allocation.

  • TU coalitional games and compact representations
  • core constraints, blocking coalitions, and empty-core examples
  • Shapley value, Shapley-Shubik, Banzhaf, and voting power

descriptionOpen notes

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • A transferable-utility coalitional game is (N, v), where v(S) assigns value to every coalition.
  • An allocation is efficient if the players' payoffs sum to v(N).
  • The core is the set of efficient allocations where every coalition receives at least its own value, so no coalition can block.
  • The core may be empty; convex games have nonempty core.
  • The Shapley value is expected marginal contribution over all random player arrival orders.
  • Its axioms are efficiency, symmetry, dummy player, and additivity; naive computation is exponential.
  • Simple voting games are winning/losing games. Shapley-Shubik measures pivotal probability in an ordering; Banzhaf counts critical coalitions.

Remember: the core asks "can a coalition walk away?"; Shapley asks "what is a fair average contribution?"

Module 5

Auction mechanisms, bidding, revenue equivalence, optimal single-item auctions, Myerson, and combinatorial auctions

An auction is a mechanism: an allocation rule plus a payment rule plus a bidding language. The oral exam usually asks you to compare incentives first, then mention revenue and computational limits.

Committee priorityMaximum focus

Auctions are a committee-friendly bridge from strategic agents to mechanisms, with clear places to test truthfulness, revenue, and computational hardness.

  • first-price, second-price, English, Dutch, and revenue equivalence assumptions
  • Myerson virtual values and optimal reserve-price intuition
  • combinatorial auctions, VCG, and winner determination hardness
65-85 min

Auction and Revenue - Learn IOE (9:17)

Say this out loud

What you must be able to say

  • An auction mechanism specifies allocation rule, payment rule, and bidding language.
  • Desirable properties include efficiency, incentive compatibility, individual rationality, revenue, and computational tractability.
  • Second-price/Vickrey auctions make truthful bidding weakly dominant in private-value single-item settings.
  • First-price sealed-bid auctions require bid shading; with n symmetric risk-neutral bidders and uniform [0,1] values, b(v)=(n-1)v/n.
  • English is strategically like second-price under private values; Dutch is strategically like first-price.
  • Revenue equivalence needs risk neutrality, independent private values, symmetric bidders, same allocation rule, and same lowest-type utility.
  • Myerson maximizes expected revenue by allocating to the highest nonnegative virtual value and charging a threshold payment; for i.i.d. regular distributions this is a reserve-price Vickrey auction.
  • Combinatorial auctions allocate bundles. Winner determination maximizes total reported value over disjoint bundles and is NP-hard in general; VCG is truthful and efficient but can have computational and revenue issues.

Remember: first ask who wins, then how much they pay, then whether truthful bidding is safe.

Two-player games and minimax

Committee priorityHeavy focus

Reported AI/ZUI questions overlap directly with MAS zero-sum game-tree reasoning, so this drill is a likely oral detour from minimax or extensive form.

  • game-state representation, terminal utilities, and winning criteria
  • minimax, alpha-beta pruning, and depth-limited evaluation
  • negamax variants and when MCTS replaces exhaustive search

Context: Bachelor AI/ZUI reports asked about two-player games, what counts as winning, minimax, alpha-beta, negamax/negascout, MCTS, game-tree storage, limited-depth search, and evaluation functions.

Show model answer

Model the game by states, player-to-move, legal actions, transition/result function, terminal test, and utility/payoff. Winning can mean reaching a terminal win/loss/draw state, maximizing utility, or guaranteeing at least the game value under optimal opponent play.

  • Minimax backs values up a game tree: MAX chooses the child with highest value, MIN chooses the lowest; in zero-sum games this gives optimal play against an optimal opponent.
  • Alpha-beta keeps bounds alpha and beta and prunes branches that cannot change the backed-up decision; move ordering controls practical speed.
  • Large games cannot store the full tree, so implementations generate nodes on demand and store only search frontier, transposition table entries, or cached evaluated states.
  • Limited-depth search stops at a cutoff and uses an evaluation function estimating utility from features such as material, mobility, threats, or goal distance.
  • Negamax is a compact zero-sum minimax form; negascout/principal-variation search improves alpha-beta in good ordering. MCTS samples a partial tree when branching is huge or evaluations are hard.

45-minute panic pass

Say these in order

  1. Normal form: define (N,A_i,u_i), pure/mixed strategies, dominance, Nash, and the warning that Nash is not welfare optimal.
  2. Zero-sum: state minimax and the LP idea; mention general Nash computation is much harder.
  3. Alternatives: correlated equilibrium is mediated recommendation; Stackelberg is leader commitment.
  4. Extensive form: define tree, information sets, perfect information, perfect recall, strategy types, SPE, backward induction, sequence form, and CFR.
  5. Game-tree bridge: minimax backs up adversarial values; alpha-beta prunes safely; depth-limited play needs evaluation.
  6. Coalitions: define (N,v), core constraints, Shapley axioms/formula intuition, and voting power indices.
  7. Auctions: allocation/payment/bidding language, truthfulness of second price, shading in first price, revenue equivalence assumptions, Myerson reserve idea, combinatorial winner determination.

Oral exam skeleton

A clean 8-minute answer

1. Frame MAS. "This topic studies several autonomous agents whose utilities interact. The key is to choose the representation: normal form, extensive form, coalitional game, or auction mechanism."

2. Normal form. "For simultaneous games I define players, actions, utilities, pure and mixed strategies, dominated strategies, and Nash equilibrium. Nash is unilateral stability, not social optimality."

3. Computation and alternatives. "Zero-sum two-player games are solved by LP and minimax. General Nash computation is difficult. Correlated equilibrium uses recommendations and is LP-computable; Stackelberg uses leader commitment."

4. Extensive form. "Sequential games use a game tree, chance, terminal utilities, and information sets. Perfect recall lets behavioral and mixed strategies match. SPE plus backward induction handles perfect information; sequence form and regret/CFR handle larger zero-sum EFGs."

5. Game-tree and planning bridge. "For deterministic adversarial games, minimax backs up max and min choices; alpha-beta prunes branches that cannot change the value. For multi-agent planning, centralized joint-action planning quickly explodes, while decentralized planning needs coordination or communication assumptions."

6. Coalitions. "A TU coalitional game assigns value to coalitions. The core is stability against blocking coalitions; the Shapley value is fair expected marginal contribution; Shapley-Shubik and Banzhaf measure voting power."

7. Auctions. "Auctions are mechanisms. Second-price is truthful; first-price shades; English and Dutch match those strategically under private values. Revenue equivalence needs strong assumptions. Myerson uses virtual values. Combinatorial auctions are expressive but winner determination is NP-hard."
